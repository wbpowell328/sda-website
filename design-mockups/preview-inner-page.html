Sequential Decision Analytics

Sequential decision analytics (SDA) is the science of making decisions, over time, in the presence of different forms of uncertainty. The decisions may be choices about resources (how much, where, when), pricing, contracts, or actions of an autonomous system. The uncertainty may come from the environment, from imperfect knowledge of the underlying processes, or from the actions of other decision-makers.

SDA brings together threads from stochastic optimization, optimal control, reinforcement learning, simulation optimization, and online learning under a single modeling framework — one that lets us see clearly which kinds of policies fit which kinds of problems.

The universal modeling framework

Every sequential decision problem can be modeled with five elements:

  • State variables — what is known at each point in time
  • Decision variables — what we choose
  • Exogenous information — what arrives that we don't control
  • Transition function — how the system evolves
  • Objective function — what we're optimizing

Four classes of policies

Within this framework, all policies (methods for making decisions) fall into one of four classes:

  1. Policy Function Approximations (PFAs) — analytic functions of the state
  2. Cost Function Approximations (CFAs) — optimizing modified costs
  3. Value Function Approximations (VFAs) — Bellman / dynamic programming
  4. Direct Lookahead Approximations (DLAs) — approximate the future
The choice of policy class is determined by the structure of the problem, not by ideology. Most real applications use hybrids drawing from two or more classes.
